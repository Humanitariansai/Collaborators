
# Daily Progress Report – November 25, 2025

## Overview
Today I focused on **Bimride’s Real-Time Driver Supply Positioning & Heatmap Intelligence System** — the part of the platform that decides *where drivers should be*, *when they should move*, and *how supply should be balanced dynamically across Barbados*. Unlike surge pricing, which is a downstream effect, supply positioning is upstream: it predicts demand before it happens and helps drivers be in the right place at the right time.

Mobility supply is a balancing act:
- Too many drivers in one zone → long idle times, low earnings  
- Too few drivers in another zone → long wait times, cancellations, unhappy riders  
- Mismatched supply → collapse of liquidity  

Barbados makes this more complicated because:
- demand is extremely **time-sensitive** (work commute, school traffic, nightlife)  
- **tourist hotspots** fluctuate depending on events, cruise ship arrivals, and weather  
- the island’s geography forces traffic into **corridors**, not grids  
- demand spikes near **beaches**, **South Coast**, **Holetown**, and **airport arrivals**  
- local culture means sudden micro-jams around rum shops, markets, and bus stands  
- rural supply is sparse but demand appears in bursts  

Today I engineered a holistic system that combines predictive modeling, geospatial clustering, incentive routing, live demand ingestion, tourist behavior modeling, and driver-app nudges to maintain real-time balance.

## Activities

### 1. Built the Barbados Demand Prediction Engine
Demand is not random — it has patterns.

I modeled:
- hour-of-day demand curves  
- weekday vs weekend behavior  
- weather sensitivity  
- event-based demand (sports, parties, cultural events)  
- cruise ship docking schedules  
- airport arrival clusters  
- tourist hotel peak times  

Barbados is one of the few places where **cruise ship arrival time** directly correlates with spikes in South Coast and Bridgetown requests.  
The model incorporates these signals.

---

### 2. Created geospatial hex-based heatmaps
The island is divided into:
- micro-hexes (high resolution)  
- macro-hexes (regional aggregation)  

Each hex stores:
- historical demand  
- predicted demand  
- live driver supply  
- pickup difficulty index  
- safety index  
- average ETAs  
- idle-time projections  

Heatmaps update every 10–15 seconds.

---

### 3. Designed Live Supply Map for internal operations
The internal map shows:
- active drivers  
- idle drivers  
- driver positions relative to hotspots  
- moving supply corridors  
- imbalance alerts  

This helps operations monitor real-time supply quality.

---

### 4. Built Driver Repositioning Nudges
Drivers receive contextual nudges like:
- “More trips are likely in Oistins in 10 minutes.”  
- “Holetown demand rising — 4 drivers needed near Limegrove.”  
- “Airport arrivals increasing soon — consider relocating.”  

Nudges depend on:
- driver score  
- distance  
- fuel considerations  
- current traffic  
- incentive eligibility  

These suggestions feel helpful, not commanding.

---

### 5. Modeled Smart Idling Zones
Drivers often wait in random spots that are inefficient.

System highlights:
- safe, well-lit idling nodes  
- shaded parking areas (hot Caribbean sun)  
- fuel-efficient idling spots  
- optimal positions within 1–2 minutes of expected demand  

These zones reduce idle time and improve pickup speed.

---

### 6. Built Tourist-Flow Prediction Engine
Tourist behavior is predictable:

- Cruise tourists move along narrow funnels between port → beaches → nightlife  
- Hotel clusters generate demand at meal times and sunset hours  
- Rain shifts tourists indoors, changing demand shape instantly  

The engine uses:
- hotel occupancy patterns  
- historical movement flows  
- weather feed  
- transportation patterns from past days  

This produces demand spikes with high reliability.

---

### 7. Integrated Weather Impact Modeling
Barbados weather changes fast.

When rain starts:
- rider demand jumps  
- driver willingness drops  
- ETAs increase  
- coastal traffic slows  
- pickups shift away from exposed areas  

System shifts drivers *before* rain hits based on radar forecasts.

### 8. Created the Supply-Imbalance Detection System
Imbalance is detected using:
- rider request density  
- driver idle density  
- ETA inflation  
- cancellation probability  
- historical comparison  

If imbalance persists:
- incentives trigger  
- nudges surface  
- pricing adjusts  
- supply movement encouraged  

Everything syncs to maintain liquidity.

---

### 9. Built Incentive Synergy with Heatmaps
Incentives aren’t random — they’re targeted.

Example:
- If South Coast is short 12 drivers at 6–8 PM → “South Coast Boost Zone: +$6 per trip for next hour.”  
- If airport arrivals surge → airport incentives activate  
- If Holetown nightlife peaks → Holetown Busy Zone bonus  

Drivers earn more *and* supply improves.

### 10. Designed Driver Pooling Logic for Thin Areas
Rural areas like St. Lucy or St. George have sparse supply.

System:
- predicts sporadic demand  
- clusters drivers nearby  
- alerts drivers when high-likelihood trip appears  
- minimizes deadhead miles (unpaid movement)  

Drivers waste less time.

---

### 11. Added Smart Routing for Supply Redistribution
When idle:
- drivers receive direction suggestions that incorporate traffic  
- repositioning routes avoid roundabout-heavy zones  
- prediction ensures drivers move *toward* future hotspots, not current ones  

The goal: get ahead of demand, not chase it.

Each type triggers different responses.
