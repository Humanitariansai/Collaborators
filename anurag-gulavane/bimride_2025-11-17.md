
# Daily Progress Report – November 17, 2025

## Overview
Today I focused on **Bimride’s Routing Engine**, specifically the architecture behind real-time navigation, ETA prediction, dynamic path correction, and Barbados‑specific routing intelligence. Routing may appear to be as simple as “use Google Maps,” but for a mobility platform, real routing must be **predictive**, **context‑aware**, **low-latency**, and **deeply integrated** with the ingestion system, pricing, safety, driver behavior scoring, and trip lifecycle orchestration.

Barbados has unique routing challenges:
- Narrow coastal roads frequently congested by buses or tourist vans  
- Roundabouts everywhere (Warrens, Graeme Hall, Six Roads, etc.)  
- Sudden mini‑traffic jams near rum shops, markets, and beaches  
- Extremely uneven traffic patterns between day and night  
- Frequent road closures for cultural events, festivals, and parades  
- Tourists stopping mid‑road to take photos  
- GPS distortion near coasts  
- Rural shortcuts that locals know but maps don’t prioritize  

Bimride cannot rely solely on off‑the‑shelf routing solutions because **the island behaves differently** from large metropolitan road networks. Today I designed a hybrid routing engine tailored specifically for Barbados.

## Activities

### 1. Broke down what “routing” actually means
A true mobility routing engine must handle:

1. **Pathfinding**  
2. **ETA prediction**  
3. **Traffic weighting**  
4. **Historical travel patterns**  
5. **Driver familiarity**  
6. **Road condition risk scoring**  
7. **Pickup maneuver planning**  
8. **Dropoff optimization**  

Each component contributes to a smooth experience.

---

### 2. Designed a hybrid graph model for Barbados
The island’s road network is not large but highly irregular.  
I built:

- A weighted directed graph of all drivable roads  
- Node attributes: lighting, safety score, road width  
- Edge attributes: speed curve, turn angle, traffic patterns  
- Biasing heuristics for tourist-heavy zones  

This hybrid graph lets Bimride compute routes differently than Google Maps by prioritizing:
- safer roads  
- easier pickup spots  
- fewer sharp turns  
- fewer blind corners  

This improves both safety and UX.

---

### 3. Built historical speed‑curve modeling
Barbados traffic varies massively by:
- cruise ship schedules  
- airport arrivals  
- weather  
- weekend nightlife  
- school traffic  
- local festivals  

I constructed a time‑bucket model:
- every road segment has a 24‑hour × 7‑day speed distribution  
- routing engine adjusts expected travel time based on this curve  

Example:
- Airport → Oistins is extremely slow at 4–6 PM  
- Holetown → Speightstown slows on weekends around beach traffic  

This makes ETA predictions far more accurate.

---

### 4. Integrated ingest pipeline with routing
Every new driver location update:
- adjusts local segment speed estimates  
- updates regional congestion  
- recalculates ETAs for other trips  
- triggers re-routing if needed  

Routing becomes **alive**, responding to real-time movement.

---

### 5. Designed “pickup-side routing”
The pickup moment is the most fragile part of any trip.

The routing engine:
- decides which side of the road to approach  
- suggests legal, safe pickup spots  
- avoids U‑turn requirements whenever possible  

For example:
Instead of telling a driver to make a blind U‑turn on the South Coast Road, Bimride will lead them around to a safer entry point.

---

### 6. Built “local knowledge shortcuts”
Barbadian drivers know shortcuts:
- small residential lanes  
- lesser-used backroads  
- beach-adjacent lanes with low traffic  

Instead of ignoring these:
- routing engine stores segments frequently chosen by top-rated drivers  
- machine learning weights them higher over time  

Crowdsourced routing becomes a competitive advantage.

---

### 7. Modeled tourist‑friendly routing
Tourists often feel unsafe on:
- narrow poorly lit roads  
- alley shortcuts at night  
- aggressive roundabout approaches  

At night or for newer drivers:
- routing engine biases toward safer, well-lit roads  
- reduces complexity  
- prefers wider, more predictable roads  

Safety influences pathfinding.

---

### 8. Integrated U-turn avoidance & roundabout intelligence
Barbados has MANY roundabouts.
New drivers and tourists struggle with them.

Routing engine:
- penalizes U-turns heavily  
- penalizes “third exit” choices unless necessary  
- favors two-exit or straight-through choices  

This improves rider comfort and safety.

---

### 9. Built ETA reliability scoring
Routing engine not only gives ETA — it gives confidence score.

Factors:
- GPS distortion probability  
- weather conditions  
- time-of-day traffic volatility  
- driver familiarity score  
- segment historical variance  

High volatility → app warns rider proactively:
“Traffic conditions may cause minor delays.”

Transparency increases trust.

---

### 10. Designed heatmap-aware routing
Demand heatmaps + routing = optimal traffic distribution.

If a driver is near a hot zone and ending a ride:
- route ending is weighted to leave driver pointed toward demand  
- reduces idle time  
- increases driver earnings  

Routing becomes operationally intelligent.

---

### 11. Modeled rural-nighttime safety adjustments
Some rural roads:
- too dark  
- too narrow  
- have unreliable GPS  

At night, routing engine:
- downgrades rural shortcuts  
- avoids plantation backroads  
- sticks to major A/B roads unless absolutely necessary  

Safety > speed for nighttime rural segments.

---

### 12. Connected routing engine to pricing fairness
If routing predicts:
- heavy turns  
- slow congestion  
- route complexity  

Then it sends metadata to pricing engine:
- adjust distance calculations  
- reduce unfair charges  
- waive wait-time fees in congestion  

Routing → pricing fairness → user satisfaction.

---

### 13. Designed fallback routing for poor connectivity
Drivers sometimes lose data.

Fallback:
- pre-computed route summary  
- low-data navigation mode  
- simplified instructions  
- cached map tiles  
- SMS-based backup instructions (future possibility)  

This ensures continuity in poor-signal areas.
